What are Question tag

short statements that are used at the end of a sentence to confirm what is being said in that particular sentence.

Examples

  • question tags like isn't it?, can you? or didn't they? 

Definition

If a statement is positive, the tag will be negative. If the statement is negative, the tag will be positive.

Types

positive question tag

positive: Original statement is positive, and the question tag is negative.

Example

  • They enjoyed the movie, didn't they?
  • He can swim well, can't he?
Negative question tag

Negative: Original statement is negative, and the question tag is positive.

Example

  • She didn't finish the book, did she?
  • We shouldn't be late, should we?
Special cases

I am - aren't I

Let's - Shall

Examples

    1. I am student, aren't I?
    2. Let's go home, Shall we?
    3. They seldom speak, do they?
    4. John and max don't like maths, do they?
    5. She is collecting stickers, isn't she?

How we use

  • Positive Statement, Negative Tag:
    If your sentence is positive, make the question tag negative.

    Example

    • You like ice cream, don't you?

  • Negative Statement, Positive Tag:
    If your sentence is negative, make the question tag positive.

    Example

    • She hasn't seen the movie, has she?

Examples

  • Affirmative statement with negative question tag:
    • You are coming, aren't you?
    • She likes coffee, doesn't she?
  • Negative statement with affirmative question tag:
    • They don't speak French, do they?
    • He hasn't finished yet, has he?
  • Imperative statement with question tag:
    • Let's go, shall we?
    • Don't forget, will you?
  • Use of modal verbs in statement and question tag:
    • He can swim, can't he?
    • She will be there, won't she?
  • Statement with "I am" and "aren't I" question tag:
    • I am right, aren't I?
    • I'm late, aren't I?

Conclusion

question tags are like mini-questions added to the end of statements. If the statement is positive, make the tag negative, and vice versa. Match the pronoun, use the right auxiliary verb, and pay attention to your tone. It's a quick way to make statements sound like questions in conversation.
